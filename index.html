Book One · Deal Structure
The Partnership Charter
David Gage, PhD — Psychologist & Business Mediator
A non-binding but comprehensive document that forces every partner — influencer, agency, reseller, co-founder — to discuss and agree on goals, governance, money, decision-making, conflict, and exit before a single contract is signed. Written in plain language. More important than the legal agreement it precedes.
Core Premise
Most partnerships fail not from bad business, but from unspoken assumptions. Gage — who spent decades mediating partnership breakdowns — found that the disputes that destroy businesses were almost always visible before they started. The charter makes the invisible visible, while partners still like each other.
Pillar One — Business Foundation
Lesson 01
Define Mission, Vision, and Values Before Anything Else
Partners must agree on the company's core purpose, competitive edge, and non-negotiable values before discussing money or roles. Gage cites Hewlett-Packard as the gold standard: HP's values were so deeply embedded that they became the spine of every decision. When the founders of Mail2000 spent 36 months aligning on their vision before raising a dollar, they sold to UPS for a rumoured $100M. The lesson: vague mission = eventual war over direction.
Lesson 02
Strategic Planning Must Expose Hidden Expectation Gaps
A good strategic plan isn't just a roadmap — it's a gap-exposure tool. Partners should build it together specifically to surface where their expectations about growth rate, risk tolerance, exit horizon, and market focus diverge. If you can't agree on 3-year goals, you cannot be aligned partners. Include: target markets, resource allocation, growth milestones, and hiring philosophy. The plan becomes the diagnostic that reveals incompatibilities early.
Lesson 03
Ownership Must Reflect Actual Contribution — Not Just Capital
Gage makes a critical distinction: control can be worth more than cash. A partner who provides the capital but leaves operations to the other may hold more equity but deserve no salary. A partner who runs the day-to-day may hold less equity but draw the salary. The charter forces a transparent conversation about what each partner actually contributes — money, time, IP, relationships, skills — and structures ownership accordingly. This prevents the most common source of partner resentment: feeling underpaid for invisible contributions.
Lesson 04
Financial Expectations Must Be Explicit and Personal
Can you go without salary for 18 months? Does your partner expect a draw from day one? These questions sound uncomfortable but are non-negotiable. Gage shows that financial misalignment — different salary expectations, different views on reinvestment vs. profit-taking, different tax strategies — is one of the top three reasons partnerships collapse. The charter creates a formal space to be blunt: what does each partner need financially, and what can the business actually provide?
Pillar Two — Governance and Decision-Making
Lesson 05
Build a Governance Framework — Even for Small Companies
Gage argues that even two-person SaaS companies benefit from board-level governance structures. A board — even one with non-owner outside directors — forces partners to rise above daily operations and assess the business strategically. Responsibilities include: long-term direction, executive succession, major financial decisions. The distinction between board-level and management-level decision-making prevents partners from confusing "we're equal partners" with "I need to approve every decision."
Lesson 06
Pre-Define Who Decides What — and at What Threshold
The charter must specify decision-making authority by category and dollar amount. Who can sign a contract unilaterally? Who needs the other's approval for a hire? Who can pivot the product strategy? Undefined authority creates either paralysis (both must approve everything) or resentment (one partner makes decisions the other feels blind-sided by). Map each decision type, set approval thresholds, and agree in advance on tie-breaking mechanisms for deadlocks.
Pillar Three — Interpersonal Dynamics
Lesson 07
Assess Leadership Styles Before They Collide in a Crisis
Arthur Sculley — who worked alongside his siblings at PepsiCo, Apple, and H.J. Heinz — told Gage that successful partnerships depend entirely on the willingness to accept the unique characteristics of others. The charter includes leadership style assessment: Are you a delegator or a controller? Do you decide fast or deliberate long? Are you comfortable with confrontation or do you avoid it? Where partners differ on these dimensions, they must agree in advance on how those differences will be managed rather than suppressed.
Lesson 08
Write a Personal Partnership Manual
Each partner should write a section that tells the other how to work with them effectively: communication preferences, triggers to avoid, commitments they value, feedback styles they respond to. This is not soft — it is operationally critical. The Ben & Jerry's near-collapse (pre-dawn frostbite and operational chaos) is cited as a case where personal incompatibilities almost ended the brand. Most partner breakdowns begin as communication breakdowns, not business disagreements.
Pillar Four — Monitoring and Health
Lesson 09
Treat Partnership Health Like Employee Performance Management
One of the top practical lessons from the book: use the same tools you'd use to manage employees to manage the partnership itself. Regular performance feedback, clear deliverables for each partner role, explicit KPIs, and scheduled "partnership health" reviews. Most partners review their business metrics but never review their partnership dynamics. Gage argues these should happen on a formal calendar — quarterly at minimum — before a crisis forces the conversation.
Lesson 10
Have the Conflict Resolution Process Ready Before You Need It
The charter must include a pre-agreed escalation ladder for disputes: 1) Direct partner discussion. 2) Neutral facilitator. 3) Mediation. 4) Binding arbitration. The reason this must be written during good times is that partners cannot agree on a fair process during a conflict — everyone believes their preferred process favours them. The Rosie O'Donnell / Gruner & Jahr magazine disaster is cited: no agreed conflict mechanism meant a brutal public lawsuit that destroyed both parties.
Pillar Five — Future Planning and Exit
Lesson 11 + 12
Plan the Future and the Exit Simultaneously
Gage insists the charter address two uncomfortable questions upfront: What does the future look like if everything goes right? (growth plans, potential acquisition interest, IPO intent, lifestyle business vs. scale ambition) and What happens if a partner wants or needs to leave? Buyout formulas, vesting structures, death/disability provisions, non-compete terms, and business continuation plans. Without this, the departure of one partner — even amicably — can destroy a company. The charter transforms a potential death event into a managed transition.

Applied to influencer/agency deals: The charter framework maps directly onto SaaS–influencer or SaaS–agency agreements. Define: What does "success" mean for this partnership? Who owns the audience relationship if the partnership ends? What happens to co-created content? Who can terminate, and on what grounds? What performance thresholds trigger renegotiation? Write this before the deal launches — not when it's falling apart.

The LockedIn Force Application

→
Before signing any influencer or agency deal: write a charter covering mission alignment (do they actually believe in discipline as a category?), financial expectations (flat fee vs. performance commission vs. equity?), decision rights (can they modify the brand message?), and exit terms (what happens to content and audience data when the deal ends?).
→
Add governance: who reviews performance, how often, and what metrics trigger renegotiation. Monthly Force Score growth targets from partner-driven installs? Minimum engagement thresholds?
→
Address the interpersonal: How does the influencer handle brand criticism? What are their communication preferences? What content decisions do they own vs. need approval for?

Book Two · Growth Engine
Ecosystem-Led Growth
Bob Moore — CEO of Crossbeam, 3x founder (RJMetrics, Stitch — both acquired)
The blueprint for transforming a partner ecosystem from a passive referral list into an active, data-driven revenue engine. Moore argues that traditional growth motions — outbound sales, inbound content, paid ads, PLG — are all declining in efficiency. The companies winning in SaaS today use partner data to find, close, and expand customers faster than any solo channel can.
The Founding Story and Core Warning
Moore built RJMetrics into a high-growth analytics company — then watched it get wiped out by AWS Redshift, Looker, and the rise of best-of-breed tech stacks. The lesson he draws: companies that try to be all-in-one solutions get outcompeted by ecosystems of specialised partners. He sold RJMetrics for parts. Looker — which built a deep partner ecosystem — sold to Google for $2.6 billion. That contrast is the engine of the entire book.
Part One — Why Traditional Growth Is Failing
Lesson 01
Outbound, Inbound, Paid, and PLG Are All at Peak Saturation
Moore documents that all four major SaaS growth motions have hit diminishing returns simultaneously: outbound is drowned by inbox noise; inbound content is commoditised by AI; paid CAC has risen beyond sustainable LTV ratios; PLG requires a product-qualified motion that most B2B tools can't self-serve. In a zero-interest-rate era these motions looked efficient. Post-ZIRP, they're not. The next growth layer has to come from relationships that pre-exist the sales conversation — i.e., partner ecosystems.
Lesson 02
Partnerships Have Been Kept Alive by Fear, Not Value
Moore delivers a blunt diagnosis: "Historically, partnerships are kept around not because of what they deliver by existing but because of the fear of what would happen if they did not exist at all." Most SaaS companies have a partner program that consists of a partner portal nobody uses, a PDF of commission rates, and a Slack channel with 12 members. This isn't a strategy. ELG is about turning that cost centre into a revenue engine by making partner data operational — not just relational.
Part Two — The ELG Vocabulary (Critical Definitions)
Lesson 03
An Ecosystem Is a Network of Entities Working Toward Shared Customer Value
Moore's definition is precise: an ecosystem is a network of interlinked companies, communities, and individuals that collectively provide advantages to shared customers. Not just "companies you integrate with." The key word is shared customers — the entire ELG playbook is built on the insight that you and your partners already have overlapping customer bases. That overlap is the data goldmine. An integration partner who serves your same ICP is not a nice-to-have — they are a sales intelligence asset.
Lesson 04
Account Overlaps Are the Core Unit of ELG Value
Account mapping — comparing your customer list with a partner's customer list — is the foundational play in ELG. Overlaps reveal three high-value signals: 1) Prospects who are already customers of your partner (warm intro possible). 2) Partners' customers who aren't yet yours (inbound opportunity with social proof pre-loaded). 3) Shared customers who use both products (expansion and retention leverage). Moore's platform Crossbeam automates this. Manually, it starts with a shared spreadsheet. Either way, it starts here.
Lesson 05
The Ecosystem Qualified Lead (EQL) Is the New MQL
Moore introduces the EQL as the most important new lead type in modern SaaS: a prospect that has been qualified not just by their own firmographics or intent signals, but by their position in your partner ecosystem. EQLs know which of your partners they already use, whether they're in an active deal cycle with a partner, and whether they're a significant customer for someone in your network. EQLs close faster, at higher ACVs, and with better retention — because they arrive with trust pre-built via the partner relationship.
Lesson 06
Second-Party Data Is the Unfair Advantage
First-party data = what you know about your own customers. Third-party data = bought data (declining quality). Second-party data = your partner's first-party data, shared with you. This is the ELG data layer. When a partner shares which of their customers are using their product heavily, which are up for renewal, and which are evaluating complementary tools — that's sales intelligence you cannot buy, cannot infer, and cannot replicate through any solo motion. Platforms like Crossbeam provide the privacy-safe infrastructure for this exchange.
Part Three — The 4 ELG Playbooks
Lesson 07
Playbook 1: Ecosystem Development — Populate Your Partner Ecosystem with Winners
Not all partners are equal — you need a partner prioritisation matrix. Moore's framework evaluates partners on two axes: audience overlap (how many of their customers match your ICP?) and partnership investment (how committed are they to jointly driving revenue?). The quadrant produces four categories: Supernodes (high overlap, high investment — invest heavily), Tier 2 (high overlap, low investment — nurture to upgrade), Co-sellers (low overlap, high investment — useful tactically), and Long Tail (low on both — automate and don't manually manage).

Curate data access carefully: don't give every partner full visibility into your customer data. Map data-sharing agreements to partnership tier. High-value partners get rich overlap data; early-stage partners get aggregate signals only.
Lesson 08
Playbook 2: Ecosystem-Led Marketing — Fill Your Funnel with EQLs
Three specific tactics Moore covers in depth:

1. Upgrade SDRs to PDRs (Partner Development Representatives). Instead of cold outreach, PDRs use partner overlap data to identify warm prospects, then reach out through the partner relationship as the warm intro vector. Moore gives examples of companies that replaced their entire cold-outbound function with PDRs and saw conversion rates multiply.

2. Account-Based Marketing with second-party data. Companies like Everflow and RollWorks use partner overlap data to hyper-personalise ABM campaigns — knowing not just the company's size but which tools they already use.

3. Turn investors into pipeline generation engines. Moore argues that your VC investors often have portfolio company overlaps with your ICP. Structuring regular intros from investors to portfolio companies is an under-utilised EQL source.

4. Events strategy reimagined: Instead of hosting or attending broad conferences, use overlap data to identify which events your highest-overlap partners attend — then co-sponsor or co-host exclusively at those events.
Lesson 09
Playbook 3: Ecosystem-Led Sales — Close Bigger, Better Customers Faster
The Fivetran case study is the gold standard here. Fivetran's AE Playbook dashboard shows each Account Executive which partner AEs share the most account overlaps with them — enabling rep-to-rep relationship building that turns abstract partnership agreements into active co-selling. Results: deals involving partner overlap close 46% faster.

The ELG Sales Tetrahedron (from Braze's David Goldstein): Four elements required for sales team buy-in: 1) Tools, 2) Data, 3) Stakeholder engagement, and 4) Cumulative impact tracking. All four must be present or the sales team won't use the ELG layer.

Co-selling mechanics: A partner champion at their company vouches for you in deals where they're already a trusted vendor. Moore shows that warm co-sell introductions have dramatically higher win rates than any cold-entry sales motion. The key operational requirement: your sales team must have real-time visibility into which of their open opportunities have partner overlaps.

Hyperscaler Cloud Marketplaces (AWS, Azure, GCP): Moore argues these are underutilised by most SaaS companies. Large enterprises have committed spend buckets on cloud marketplaces. Being listed means your product can be purchased against pre-committed budget — removing procurement friction entirely.
Lesson 10
Playbook 4: Ecosystem-Led Customer Success — Eliminate Churn and Grow Accounts
Moore shows that ELG's highest-leverage application is actually retention, not acquisition. Bombora increased its customer revenue retention from a concerning number to over 130% NRR after implementing ELG-driven customer success.

Key mechanics: Customer Success Managers use "Upcoming Renewals with Overlaps" reports to see which renewing customers are deeply embedded in the partner ecosystem — making churn far more expensive for the customer. Customers who use multiple integrated tools in a stack have dramatically lower churn rates. This makes ELG a direct retention lever.

Tech partner-driven expansion: When a customer adds a new tool to their stack that integrates with yours, that's an expansion signal. Partner data surfaces these signals before the customer even contacts your team.

The LockedIn Force Application

→
Map overlaps immediately. Which productivity, wellness, or creator tools share the same ICP as LockedIn Force? Notion users, Pomodoro timer users, Atomic Habits readers — build overlap lists. Anyone who already sells to the "I want to be more disciplined" ICP is a potential partner.

Convert influencers from SDRs to PDRs. Instead of "post and link," structure influencer agreements around warm audience activation — their audience already knows and trusts them. That's second-party data in human form.

Book Three · Market Positioning
Crossing the Chasm
Geoffrey A. Moore — Management consultant, Venture Partner
First published 1991, still described as "the bible for entrepreneurial marketing" by Stanford in 2006, with 1M+ copies sold. The definitive framework for understanding why innovative products stall — and the exact strategies to transition from a small base of enthusiastic early adopters to the dominant mainstream market. More relevant to modern SaaS than any book published this decade.
The Core Claim
The technology adoption lifecycle is not a smooth bell curve — there is a dangerous, frequently fatal gap between Early Adopters (visionaries) and the Early Majority (pragmatists). Most companies that fail don't run out of money or ideas. They mistake early-adopter enthusiasm for mainstream demand, exhaust their resources chasing the wrong buyer profile, and fall into the chasm. The book is entirely about how not to do that.
Part One — The Five Buyer Psychographics
Profile 01
Innovators — Technology Enthusiasts
Who they are: The first 2.5% of the market. They seek out new technology for its own sake and will tolerate bugs, incomplete features, and rough UX for the privilege of being first. What they want: Raw access to the technology, a direct line to the engineers, and low/free pricing. Value to you: Useful for product validation, debugging, and initial social proof. Danger: Their feedback optimises for power features that pragmatists will never use. Don't mistake their enthusiasm for market signal.
Profile 02
Early Adopters — Visionaries
Who they are: The 13.5% after innovators. They see technology as a means to a competitive leap — they want to use your product before competitors do. Kennedy and the space program. Henry Ford and mass production. What they want: A compelling vision of transformation, customisation, and a direct relationship with the vendor. They don't need references. They don't need the product to be complete. Danger: They are terrible references for pragmatists. A visionary raving about your product actually makes pragmatists more suspicious, not less.
Profile 03
Early Majority — Pragmatists (The Chasm Gatekeepers)
Who they are: The 34% that make you a real business. They are deeply practical, risk-averse, and highly social in their buying decisions. What they want: Proven solutions. Industry references (not visionary references — they want to hear from people exactly like them). Compatible with their existing stack. A full ecosystem of supporting vendors. The critical insight: Pragmatists don't buy until they can compare — they need to see competitive alternatives. You may have to actively create your competition to cross the chasm. Positioning that works: Not "state-of-the-art" but "industry standard."
Profile 04
Late Majority — Conservatives
Who they are: Another 34%. They adopt when something becomes the established standard — and even then, only from large, well-established companies with proven support. What they want: Single-focus products that do one thing well. Low price. No disruption. High convenience. They want evolution, not revolution. What they don't want: To debug anything, to need IT involvement, or to explain the purchase to leadership. Moore's note: They waited long enough that prices have dropped to just above cost. They'll never be high-margin accounts. Design for them last.
Profile 05
Laggards — Skeptics
Who they are: The last 16%. They do not participate in the high-tech marketplace — except to block purchases. Moore's verdict: "The primary function of high-tech marketing in relation to skeptics is to neutralise their influence." They're not your customer. They may be inside your customer's organisation trying to kill your deal. Know them not as buyers but as obstacles, and address their objections through social proof and risk reduction rather than feature persuasion.
The Fundamental Gap
Why Visionaries Cannot Reference Pragmatists
Moore identifies four specific traits of visionaries that actively alienate pragmatists: 1) Visionaries don't respect the value of colleagues' experience (pragmatists do). 2) Visionaries are seeking competitive advantage through exclusivity — they don't want you to sell to anyone else yet. 3) They expect customisation and bespoke features. 4) They have project-driven budgets, not operational ones. A pragmatist reads a visionary reference and thinks: "That person wanted something risky and radical. That's not me." The reference backfires.
Part Two — Crossing the Chasm Strategy
Lesson 06
The Beachhead Strategy — Big Fish, Small Pond
Moore's central strategic prescription is the beachhead market: identify the smallest possible vertical segment of pragmatists where you can achieve complete dominance — then use that dominance as the launchpad into adjacent segments.

The analogy is D-Day: the Allied invasion didn't try to land everywhere at once. It focused overwhelming force on one beach (Normandy), established a secured position, then expanded outward. Tesla did this with luxury EVs before moving to mass market. You do it by picking one job title, in one industry vertical, with one use case, and dominating it before expanding.

Why this works for crossing the chasm: within a tight vertical, pragmatists talk to each other. A single win with a respected company in that vertical creates the reference base that makes all subsequent sales faster. Without this, you have disconnected wins with no cross-referencing community, no word-of-mouth, and no evidence of "industry standard" status.

The criteria for a good beachhead: 1) A compelling reason to buy (a pain point that's urgent, not nice-to-have). 2) A referenceable customer base (decision-makers who talk to each other). 3) A complete-able market (small enough to win, large enough to launch from). 4) Compatibility with your product's genuine strengths.
Lesson 07
The Whole Product — Pragmatists Buy Solutions, Not Features
Pragmatists don't buy a product — they buy a complete solution to a problem. Moore's "Whole Product" concept: the generic product is just the core. The whole product includes: complementary products, integrations, professional services, support, training, and community. Early adopters can integrate the product themselves. Pragmatists will not. Before crossing the chasm, you must assemble the whole product — either by building it, partnering for it, or acquiring it. The partner ecosystem is therefore not optional for crossing the chasm — it is a precondition.
Lesson 08
Positioning That Works for Each Psychographic
Enthusiasts: Name it and frame it. Tell them what it is technically. Visionaries: Tell them who it's for and what the transformational impact could be — they'll extrapolate the vision. Pragmatists: Show competitive landscape and differentiation. Who else is buying? What do I compare it against? Conservatives: Show financial stability and future roadmap. Is this company safe to bet on? The fatal mistake most startups make: using visionary positioning language ("disrupts the entire industry," "reimagines how you work") when pitching pragmatist buyers. They hear it and think: risky.
Lesson 09
Distribution Channel Must Match the Pragmatist's Comfort Zone
Moore's often-overlooked insight: "The number-one corporate objective when crossing the chasm is to secure a distribution channel into the mainstream market, one with which the pragmatist customer will be comfortable." Pricing is not primarily for the customer — it's to motivate the channel. A channel partner needs margin to invest in selling your product. Pricing that makes sense for the customer but leaves no channel margin will produce a dead partner program. Map your distribution strategy to where pragmatist buyers actually trust and buy.
Lesson 10
Social Proof Evidence Hierarchy for Pragmatist Buyers
Moore documents exactly what pragmatists use as evidence in their buying process (in order of persuasive power): 1) Market share data — the most powerful signal. 2) Quality and number of your partner ecosystem — if you lack market share data, show your partners' commitment. 3) Industry analyst recognition (Gartner, Forrester). 4) Customer references from their specific industry vertical — not generic testimonials, but peer-to-peer references. The operating principle: "You identify leaders by their followers." Your partner ecosystem is literal social proof that you're the emerging standard.
Lesson 11
The Four Domains of Value Shift Across the Lifecycle
Moore identifies four domains that customers value: Technology, Product, Market, and Company. The emphasis shifts as buyers change:

Early market (Innovators + Visionaries): Technology and Product dominate. They want to know what the tech can do and what the product's features are. Mainstream market (Pragmatists + Conservatives): Market and Company dominate. They want to know: Is this the market leader? Is this company stable enough to be a long-term partner?

The practical implication: Your marketing message, your sales pitch, your partner positioning, and your PR strategy must shift completely when you cross the chasm. What won your early customers will actively repel mainstream buyers. The content you wrote for visionaries (feature-deep, transformation-focused) must be replaced by market-leadership content (case studies, analyst placements, partner showcases). Most companies never make this shift — they keep talking to pragmatists the way they talked to visionaries, and wonder why deals stall.

The LockedIn Force Application

→
You are currently in early-adopter territory. Your core audience — high-performance obsessives, productivity enthusiasts, self-improvement identity buyers — are visionaries. They love the concept. They'll tolerate rough edges. Use influencers who speak this language to build this base.
→
Identify your beachhead. Which one pragmatist segment has the most urgent need for enforced discipline? Founders? Medical students? Remote workers with no external accountability? Pick one. Dominate it with case studies, community, and references before expanding to adjacent segments.
→
Do not use visionary influencers to sell to pragmatists. A productivity hype-creator raving about LockedIn Force makes pragmatists trust it less. For crossing the chasm, you need industry-specific validation: "here's a verified case study from a medical student who improved their board score using this." Peer references, not visionary endorsements.
→
Build the whole product now. The pragmatist buyer won't set the app up themselves. Integration with calendar tools, Notion, Todoist — the whole product stack makes the solution complete enough for mainstream adoption.

Book Four · Influence Psychology
Influence: The Psychology of Persuasion
Dr. Robert B. Cialdini — Professor of Psychology, Arizona State University. 35 years of research.
The most scientifically rigorous book on why humans say yes — and how those principles map directly to why influencer partnerships, referral programs, co-selling, and partner-driven acquisition actually convert. First published 1984. 5M+ copies sold. Still the definitive text. Cialdini added a 7th principle (Unity) in the expanded edition. Every principle is a conversion lever you can engineer into your partner program.
The Core Premise
In a complex world, humans use mental shortcuts to make decisions. These shortcuts — "click, whirr" automatic responses — evolved because they're usually correct. But they can be triggered deliberately. Cialdini spent three years working undercover inside sales, marketing, and compliance organisations to document how professionals use these triggers. The result: 7 universal principles that operate across culture, context, and product category. Knowing them is the difference between a partner program that converts and one that just looks active.
The 7 Principles — In Full
Principle 01
Reciprocity — The Obligation to Give Back
Humans feel a powerful obligation to repay what others have given them. Critically: this obligation is triggered even by uninvited gifts, and it overrides personal liking. The Hare Krishna experiments showed that people gave donations to people they didn't like and didn't want the flower from — because they'd accepted the gift and felt obligated. Carter's legislative failures in Congress are attributed to his refusal to build reciprocal political capital — nobody owed him anything.

Applied: Go first and go positive without expectation. An influencer who receives genuine resources, access, and support from you before you ask them to promote you will feel the pull of obligation. A partner who receives a warm intro from you (before asking for one back) is far more likely to reciprocate. The Hare Krishna principle: give something the other party didn't explicitly ask for, and the pull to reciprocate is amplified.
Principle 02
Commitment and Consistency — The Need to Honour Prior Choices
Once someone makes a choice or takes a stand, they will face personal and interpersonal pressure to behave consistently with it. The foot-in-the-door technique: getting a small initial yes makes a large subsequent yes far more likely. The Drive Safely experiment showed that homeowners who had previously placed a small postcard in their window were 4x more likely to allow a large billboard — because they'd already "committed" to the cause.

The power amplifiers: voluntary (chosen freely, not coerced), active (written or publicly stated, not just thought), and public (witnessed by others). A commitment that has all three is nearly unbreakable.

Applied: Before asking an influencer for a major campaign, get small public commitments first. Ask them to post their Force Score. Ask them to take the 7-day challenge. Each small public commitment increases the likelihood of full campaign participation — and makes them feel internally consistent in continuing. The missed-appointment reduction (18%) from asking patients to write down their own appointment details is a direct analogy: get partners to write their own goals in the partnership agreement.
Principle 03
Social Proof — We Follow What Similar Others Do
Cialdini describes this as potentially the most powerful of all principles because it operates automatically and affects people even when they're aware of it. "We use what other people think is correct to determine what is correct ourselves." The hotel towel experiment: changing the card from "environmental reasons" to "75% of guests in this room have reused their towels" increased reuse by 26% — because it used peer social proof rather than abstract values.

Activation conditions: Social proof is most powerful under uncertainty, in ambiguous situations, and when the reference group is similar to the observer. A testimonial from "a business professional" is weak. A testimonial from "a founder of a 12-person SaaS company who works from home" is powerful — because it's specific and similar.

Applied: The Force Score public sharing mechanic in LockedIn Force is a direct social proof engine. "47,000 people shared their Force Score this week" is social proof. "People exactly like you are using this" is social proof. The influencer's audience watching them track and share their own score is social proof delivered by proxy.
Principle 04
Authority — We Defer to Credible Experts
Humans are conditioned to follow the lead of genuine experts. This deference is so automatic that even the symbols of authority — titles, attire, credentials, expert introductions — trigger compliance independently of actual expertise. An expert introduction before a keynote increased appointment bookings by 20% and signed contracts by 15%.

The Milgram experiments: Ordinary people administered apparently dangerous electric shocks when instructed by someone in a lab coat. The authority cue (lab coat) overrode personal moral judgment.

Applied: Who has credibility in your niche that you can associate with? A neuroscientist who validates the science of enforced focus? A high-performance coach with a verified track record? Authority is manufactured through association, credentials, and the quality of evidence — not just self-claim. In partner deals: position yourself as the category authority. Let partners borrow your authority in their content ("the #1 discipline enforcement app") — borrowed authority is still authority.
Principle 05
Liking — We Say Yes to People We Like
Three factors drive liking at near-universal scale: 1) Similarity — we like people who are like us in background, interests, values, or circumstances. 2) Compliments — genuine flattery works even when people know it's a sales technique. 3) Cooperation toward mutual goals — working together on a shared objective generates liking independent of prior relationship.

Joe Girard — the world's greatest car salesman — sent every customer a holiday card that simply said "I like you." Sales compound because of it.

Applied: This is why influencer marketing works at a fundamental level — the audience already likes the influencer. Their endorsement inherits the relationship. The implication for deal structure: don't just hire influencers as media channels. Build genuine relationships where they actually like and use the product. Authentic endorsement from a genuine fan converts at multiples of a paid promotional post. The similarity principle means micro-influencers with deeply similar audiences outperform macro-influencers with broad audiences for conversion (even if they underperform for reach).
Principle 06
Scarcity — We Want What We Might Lose Access To
Scarcity operates on two levels: 1) We use rarity as a shortcut to determine value (the expensive = good heuristic). 2) We hate losing freedoms we currently have — what psychologists call reactance. The jewellery store that doubled prices by mistake and sold out faster is the landmark example of the first. Parents banning books or drugs creating higher demand is the landmark example of the second.

Activation conditions: Newly experienced scarcity is more powerful than constant scarcity. The most powerful scarcity tactic Cialdini documents: tell customers of a limited supply and tell them that information is exclusive. A double scarcity (the thing is scarce AND the information about its scarcity is exclusive) produced the highest sales results in his research.

Competition amplification: Being in competition for a scarce resource has powerfully motivating properties. The leaderboard mechanic in LockedIn Force deploys exactly this — seeing your streak position fall (imminent loss) is a more powerful motivator than seeing it rise.

Applied: Partner access tiers. Early co-founder partner program with limited slots. Exclusive integration benefits. Time-limited onboarding support. These all leverage scarcity. The Streak mechanic leverages loss aversion scarcity — you're about to lose your streak if you don't act.
Principle 07
Unity — The Shared Identity Trigger (The 7th and Most Recent Principle)
Added in Cialdini's expanded edition — and arguably the most relevant principle for community-driven products. Unity is not about similarity (Liking) — it's about shared identity. "We is the shared me." When people feel they belong to the same group — family, ethnicity, political tribe, national identity, cultural movement — their sense of self merges with the group. Influencing a member of that group influences their identity.

The Unity principle operates at the level of: Are we the same kind of person? Not "do we have similar interests" but "are we fundamentally of the same tribe?"

Applied: "LockedIn" as a cultural identity is a direct Unity deployment. The word doesn't describe a feature — it describes a kind of person. When someone says "I'm LockedIn," they are stating tribal membership. This is why the brand language ("Are you LockedIn or just pretending?") works — it activates the Unity principle's in-group/out-group mechanics. The partner program benefits from this: influencers and agency partners who genuinely identify as "LockedIn people" will promote the product with a conviction that no paid brief can produce. Recruit for identity alignment first, audience size second.

The contrast principle (documented outside the seven but throughout the book): if the second item in a presentation is different from the first, we see it as more different than it actually is. Pitch after a worse alternative to look better. Sell the expensive item first, then the add-ons look cheap by comparison.
